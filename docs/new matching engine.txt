. The "Various Artists" Logic (The Compilation Trap)
Simple matching engines fail when they see a folder called Ministry of Sound - Annual 2024. They look for an artist named "Ministry of Sound" and fail.
The Fix: You need a specific flag in your TrackCandidate called is_compilation.
The Logic: If is_compilation is True, the Matching Engine should ignore the Album Artist for scoring purposes and match purely on Track Title + Track Artist.
Why add it now? If you don't, your engine will try to "fix" compilations by renaming them to the individual track artists, scattering a single album across 20 different folders.
2. "Release Group" vs. "Release" (The Edition Problem)
MusicBrainz distinguishes between the concept of an album (Release Group) and the specific CD (Release).
The Problem: Your user wants "Dark Side of the Moon."
Result A: 1973 Original Vinyl (9 tracks).
Result B: 2011 Remaster (9 tracks + 2 bonus live tracks).
The Fix: Your engine needs a Preferred Release Type weight.
Implementation:
If the user wants a "perfect FLAC match," the engine must penalize Result A if the file is from Result B (track counts won't match).
Add total_tracks and disc_count to your SoulSyncTrack schema. If the local folder has 12 files but the Metadata Candidate has 9, that’s a -50% score penalty.
3. Rate-Limit Caching (The Performance Saver)
You mentioned slskd downloading and Syncing. Both involve high-volume queries. Services like MusicBrainz allow 1 request per second. If you scan a 10,000-song library, it will take 3 hours.
The Fix: A Short-Term Cache Layer inside the Core.
Implementation: Before asking the Provider "Who is The Weeknd?", check a local Redis/SQLite cache.
Why add it now? If you try to bolt this on later, you will have to rewrite every find_match function. Build it into the MatchService wrapper from Day 1.
